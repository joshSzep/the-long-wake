# ARC.md

**The Long Wake**

This is not a plot arc.
It is a **state of being** arc.

The story tracks a person moving from total emotional stasis to the smallest possible form of forward motion.

## 1. Waking

The novel begins with the protagonist waking up from cryosleep too early.

He is disoriented, physically weak, and emotionally flat.

His first instinct is not curiosity or fear. It is refusal.

He tries to go back to sleep.

He believes this wake was a mistake that can be undone.

## 2. The Realization

The ship informs him that cryosleep cannot be resumed.

He is now permanently awake.

The future exists, but it is decades away.

Everyone else remains frozen.

This is when hopelessness takes root.

He is not in danger.
He is not in crisis.

He is in **limbo**.

## 3. Living Inside Waiting

Most of the book lives here.

The protagonist settles into a bleak routine:

• Eating
• Walking
• Monitoring systems
• Talking to the AI
• Watching the cryo bays

Time passes without meaning.

This section should feel heavy, repetitive, and quiet.

The reader should feel the same suspension the protagonist feels.

## 4. The First Friction

Something small disrupts the stasis.

It is not dramatic.

It might be:

• A glitch
• A message fragment
• A memory
• A question from the AI
• A sound
• A habit

For the first time, he feels the faintest sense of **pull**.

Not happiness.
Not hope.

Interest.

## 5. Choosing to Stay Awake

The protagonist does not become optimistic.

But he begins to do one new thing that he did not do before.

He chooses.

A direction.
A task.
A routine.
A connection.

Something that creates a tiny vector toward the future.

## 6. The Long Wake Continues

The book does not end with arrival.

It ends with continuation.

He is still waiting.

But now he is not only enduring.

He is leaning.

The future is still far away.

But it is no longer invisible.

That is the arc.

Not salvation.

Motion.
