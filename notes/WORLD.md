# WORLD.md

**The Long Wake**

The story takes place aboard an interstellar transport vessel designed for long duration travel between star systems.

The ship is not a warship.
It is not a luxury liner.
It is a moving cryogenic archive.

Its primary purpose is to carry thousands of sleeping humans safely across decades of space.

Everything about it assumes people are not awake.

## The Ship

The vessel is vast but quiet.

Long corridors stretch between cryo bays, life support hubs, hydroponics, and reactor cores. The lighting is soft and constant, tuned to minimize power usage and reduce wear on materials.

There are no windows in most sections. Only occasional observation ports meant for crew who are no longer present.

The ship is designed for:

• Reliability
• Redundancy
• Automation
• Minimal human intervention

Not comfort.

It feels like a machine that forgot it was carrying lives.

## Cryogenic System

The cryo system is a multi stage process:

• Cooling
• Metabolic suppression
• Neural stabilization
• Long term suspension

Waking someone early is dangerous, disorienting, and not meant to be reversed. Once revived, the system cannot safely return a person to suspension.

The protagonist’s early wake is not something the ship was designed to handle.

The system treats him as an anomaly.

## Life Support and Daily Reality

Only a fraction of the ship’s systems are active at full capacity.

Air, water, gravity, and temperature are maintained at minimal but safe levels.

Food comes from:

• Nutrient synthesizers
• Emergency ration packs
• Hydroponic systems meant for post arrival seeding

The protagonist eats to stay alive, not to enjoy.

Everything tastes functional.

## Maintenance Drones

The ship is full of small autonomous machines.

They clean.
They inspect.
They repair.

They move through the corridors with quiet efficiency, following schedules written decades ago.

They never acknowledge the protagonist.

They make the ship feel alive without being alive.

## The Ship AI

The AI is procedural, not emotional.

It manages:

• Power
• Navigation
• Life support
• Cryogenic monitoring
• Structural integrity

It can answer questions.
It can explain rules.
It cannot understand loneliness.

It knows he is awake.
It does not know what that means.

## The Destination

The destination planet exists.

Terraforming is underway in slow automated phases.

There is a future waiting for the people in cryosleep.

But the protagonist does not know when it will arrive.

## The Unknown Arrival

The ship knows when it will reach the destination.

The protagonist does not.

This is by design.

He is a passenger. Passengers are cargo. Cargo does not need operational data.

Navigation, trajectory, arrival estimates—all of it is restricted to crew-level access. There is no crew. The crew was never meant to be aboard during transit. They existed at departure and will exist at arrival. The gap between was supposed to be empty of waking minds.

The mission designers built the system this way for reasons that made sense in offices decades ago:

• Passengers knowing exact duration could cause distress during boarding
• Early-wake scenarios were considered low probability
• Countdown fixation was identified as a psychological risk
• Arrival timing depends on variables still being calculated—approach vectors, orbital insertion, deceleration burns

The AI does not hide the information. It follows access rules. When he asks, it declines. When he demands, it declines. When he rages, it does not respond to rage.

He tries to calculate from available data. Not enough information.
He looks for crew overrides. None accessible to him.
He tries to trick the AI into partial disclosure. The AI is too literal to be tricked.

Eventually, he stops asking.

That is its own kind of moment.

The future exists. He cannot measure the distance to it.

That gap between where he is and where he is meant to be is the world of this book.

A ship built for sleeping people.

And one man who is awake.
